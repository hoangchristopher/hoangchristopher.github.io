Project 2: Fun with Filters and Frequencies

In this project, we will be exploring how to analyze and manipulate the various low and high frequencies of images.

Part 1: Fun with Filters

Part 1.1: Convolutions From Scratch

To convolve over an image we need to iterate over all of the pixels in the image with a kernel. First, to take care of cases where our kernel exceeds the bounds of our image, we can incorporate zero-padding, where any value our kernel decides to operate on that is outside of the image will be 0. This can be done by surrounding the source image with layers of 0s determined by half the kernel's width and height respectively. This was done by creating a larger numpy array of 0s and placing the original image inside of it according to the kernel's dimensions.

From there, we can iterate through each of the padded image's non-padding pixels with our kernel, performing various multiplication and addition operations within the kernel's defined area. During this phase, the kernel's window is defined by slicing operations using the current pixel's coordinate and the kernel's dimensions.

During this phase, you can perform the kernel operations in one of two ways. You could either use two more for loops to iterate through the area that the kernel covers, or you could use slicing to grab the relevant indices around your current pixel to grab the values inside of your defined kernel.

Finally, we can return the resulting image represented as a matrix without zero-padding

Binarization of the image is done by assigning all pixels above a certain value as 1, while the rest are assigned 0. This helps visualize what we determine as "relevent" pixels. With a lower threshold, many specks of white may appear on the binarized image due to the various non-zero values found throughout the gradient magnitude image, so I decided to choose a threshold that could give the cameraman defined details while not including too many white specks (which was that anything greater than 0.25 would be assigned a value of 1 and 0 otherwise).

Part 1.3: Derivative of Gaussian (DoG) Filter

To try and eliminate even more of the white specks, or noise, from the result, we can apply a gassian smoothing filter to blur the image before take its derivatives. With a blurred image, there will be less high frequencies, and thus, random high frequencies throughout the image won't be able to "influence" artifacts too much and send their values over the binarization threshold.

Part 2: Fun with Frequencies!

Part 2.1: Image "Sharpening"

Here, we experiment with applying low-pass filters to images, eliminating their high frequencies and manipulating the results. Specifically, when we subtract a low-passed version of an image from its source image, we are left with all of the image's high frequencies. These high frequencies are all of the edges and prominent details of the image. With these high frequencies, we can add them back to the original image to obtain a "sharpened" version of the original image. Here is is on a picture of the Taj Mahal.

Here, we can see that the "sharper" an image gets, the more coarse and grainy it is, as all of the fluctuations in frequency are enunciated in the result.

Part 2.2: Hybrid Images

A fun thing we can do to create hybrid images is overlay the high frequencies of one image onto another image containing low frequencies. This results in an image where, if you view it from up-close, your eyes will pick up on the high frequencies and see one image, but when you view it from far away, and thus your eyes pick up on the low frequencies, you see another image.
